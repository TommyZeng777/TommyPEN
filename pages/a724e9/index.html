毛选文章解析(英文版) The Analysis of selected works Mao Zedong Thought

# 3.1 The Preface and Epilogue of the Rural Investigation

# Ⅰ. The background of history:

This piece was written in the context of the Yan’an Rectification Movement. Strictly speaking, it is not an article, but rather a preface and an epilogue composed by Mao Zedong as part of an educational material.

A preface refers to the introductory section at the beginning of a long article. An epilogue refers to a concluding section at the end of a long article. The third volume of the Selected Works opens with the preface and epilogue from Rural Investigation, which carries profound significance.

So, when we talk about rectification, what kind of style is it exactly that needs to be rectified?

At that time, there were mainly three styles: subjectivism, dogmatism, and sectarianism. All three forms of rectification referred to various aspects within the Party, including attitudes toward learning, working methods, and writing styles. Therefore, Mao Zedong also emphasized the need to rectify the Party’s work style, academic style, and rigid, formulaic writing styles that hindered clear thinking and genuine expression, among others.

  • To rectify these unhealthy tendencies, one crucial and indispensable method is to highlight and promote their opposites.
  • To combat the style of subjectivism, one must promote the spirit of conducting investigations based on practical realities.
  • To combat the style of dogmatism, one must promote the spirit of seeking truth from facts and applying theory to practical situations.
  • To combat the style of sectarianism, one must promote the spirit of criticism and self-criticism, and of unity in looking forward together.

In other words, when addressing an unhealthy tendency, it is essential to clearly point out the correct, positive tendencies. Only through such contrasts can a stronger and more profound understanding be achieved.

In the case of dogmatism and subjectivism, their best counterexamples are precisely the styles of 'seeking truth from facts' and 'integrating theory with practice.' Among these admirable work styles, the most immediate and actionable step that should be learned and implemented without delay is: start rural investigation.

Why was rural investigation emphasized? Because at that time, the areas under our control were predominantly rural. Therefore, Mao Zedong strongly advocated that leading cadres devote themselves to in-depth investigations in the countryside.

Driven by this rural investigation movement, many cadres began to truly immerse themselves in the countryside to investigate all aspects of rural life—its current conditions, economic activities, organizational structures, and more. One of the most well-known figures in this effort was Zhang Wentian.

He was one of the well-known members of the group who had studied in the Soviet Union. During the time in the Soviet areas, Zhang Wentian played a significant role in the implementation and even initiation of certain overly leftist policies.

It was only after the rise of the rural investigation movement that his footsteps truly began to enter the countryside and connect with reality. It was at this point that he started to integrate the Marxism-Leninism he had studied in the Soviet Union with the concrete conditions of China.

This very piece serves as the preface and epilogue written for those rural investigation reports.

In order to carry out this rural investigation, it need a very perfect template in rural investigation. So Mao Zedong had several model reports on rural investigation printed and distributed, so that everyone could learn how to carry out effective rural investigations.

At that time, the printed models provided to everyone were several reports, including the Xingguo Investigation, the Changgang Township Investigation, and the Caixi Township Investigation. These reports were investigations conducted by Mao Zedong during the period of the Central Soviet Area, the early revolutionary base established by the Chinese Communist Party. They are now available online, and readers are encouraged to take some time to read them.

The Central Soviet Area refers to the early revolutionary base established by the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) during the early 1930s, particularly in regions such as Ruijin in Jiangxi Province. These areas were called “Soviet Areas” because they were modeled after the Soviet Union’s political system and served as localized governments where the CCP experimented with socialist policies, land reform, and mass mobilization. The “Central” Soviet Area was the most prominent and served as the Party’s political and military center before the Long March.

Naturally, this piece serves as the preface and epilogue to those rural investigation reports.

# Ⅱ. The preface discusses the importance of investigation in many ways, but more importantly, it emphasizes the attitude one should have toward investigation.

There are many possible methods of investigation—for example, holding inquiry meetings, conducting interviews or discussions with farmers, or directly participating in their daily lives and labor. One could also observe how production and policies are implemented in rural areas, and summarize the problems that arise during practice. All of these are valid investigative methods.

However, methods can be copied, but attitudes cannot. Without genuine dedication to investigation, it may degenerate into a cursory observation or end up being just a superficial glance, and reduce the investigation to a mere formality. Furthermore, some individuals purport to conduct an investigation, yet upon reaching rural areas, they behave like self - proclaimed experts, condescendingly criticizing the farmers as if they were dictating rules to them.

This kind of investigation method characterized by bureaucratism is seriously unacceptable and inadvisable. Methods can be improved, However, if the attitude is not right, then the investigation will turn into something of formalism, and in the end, it will be of little avail.

After the founding of the People's Republic of China, Mao Zedong also called on cadres to launch a widespread campaign of investigation and research. However, once many cadres actually reached the grassroots, it turned into mere formalism. Instead of genuinely studying problems in factories, rural areas, and society, most went just indulege in eating and drinking, to "instruct" the grassroots, to issue orders, and to put on bureaucratic airs.

Here it reflects the changes and struggles in ideology.

That is to say: If you think you are more superior to the masses, and your thoughts are all advanced and scientific, and masses are alway ignorant and backward, then you don't need to conduct an investigation, becasue it will definitely be a bureaucratic and formalistic one.

Conversely, if you believe that genuine creativity always stems from people, and that it is among the people where practice yields true knowledge, then you can truly draw nourishment from this guide and serve them.
